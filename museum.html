Adenocarcinoma Colon

16Path

Final Diagnosis

Well-differentiated Adenocarcinoma of Colon (Mucin Secreting, Papillary type)


Etiology Pathogenesis Pecularity Differentials Investigations Clinical Pearls Quiz/MCQ Credits
  • APC or Wnt Pathway Defect: The most common molecular basis of sporadic colorectal adenocarcinoma; biallelic inactivation of the APC tumor suppressor gene, a key negative regulator of Wnt signaling initiates the adenoma-carcinoma sequence in up to 80% of cases.
  • Hereditary Polyposis and Non-Polyposis Syndromes: Germline mutations in APC (FAP), MUTYH (MAP), or mismatch repair genes MLH1 or MSH2 or MSH6 or PMS2 (Lynch syndrome or HNPCC) account for approximately 10% of all colorectal cancers; Lynch syndrome alone confers up to a 17-fold elevated risk.
  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease: Longstanding ulcerative colitis with pancolitis increases cancer risk at a rate of approximately 0.5–1% per year after the first decade, with 8–30% lifetime risk; the molecular pathogenesis in this setting differs from sporadic cases, with less frequent KRAS and APC mutations.
  • High Animal Fat or Calorie-Dense Diet: Ingestion of animal fats promotes growth of anaerobic gut microbiota (Fusobacterium nucleatum,Bacteroides fragilis) that convert normal bile acids into carcinogens; high fat intake additionally elevates serum cholesterol, itself is a risk factor for colorectal adenomas.
  • Metabolic Syndrome & Insulin Resistance Obesity-driven hyperinsulinemia raises circulating insulin-like growth factor type I (IGF-I) concentrations, stimulating intestinal mucosal proliferation; diabetes mellitus independently increases colorectal cancer risk through related mechanisms.
  • Tobacco Use Cigarette smoking is linked to development of colorectal adenomas, particularly after more than 35 years of use, though no biologic mechanism has yet been.

Colorectal adenocarcinoma evolves through three partially overlapping molecular pathways, each involving stepwise accumulation of genetic and epigenetic abnormalities. In the dominant APC/β-catenin pathway, biallelic loss of APC function releases β-catenin from APC-dependent degradation, enabling it to translocate to the nucleus and form a complex with TCF, thereby activating proliferative genes including MYC and cyclin D1; sequential acquisition of activating KRAS mutations (present in 50% of adenomas greater than 1 cm and 50% of invasive adenocarcinomas) suppresses apoptosis, loss of SMAD2 and SMAD4 removes TGF-β-mediated growth inhibition, and TP53 mutations (occurring in 70–80% of frank carcinomas) permit unrestricted progression, together constituting the chromosomal instability hallmark of this pathway. In the mismatch repair (MMR) deficiency pathway, inactivation of MLH1 or MSH2 - whether by germline mutation (Lynch syndrome) or acquired promoter methylation - allows mutations to accumulate at a rate up to 1000 times above normal within microsatellite repeat regions, with consequent disruption of the type II TGF-β receptor gene (TGFBR2) and the pro- apoptotic BAX gene, both of which promote uncontrolled mucosal proliferation and clone survival; this MSI-high phenotype is preferentially associated with right-sided, mucinous tumors with peritumoral lymphocytic infiltrates. The CpG island hypermethylation phenotype (CIMP) pathway is characterized by epigenetic silencing of MLH1 via promoter hypermethylation in the absence of germline MMR mutations, frequently co-occurring with activating BRAF mutations while KRAS and TP53 remain largely intact; these tumors also tend to arise in the right colon and may be either MSI-high or microsatellite stable.

Unlike intramucosal carcinoma at any other site in the gastrointestinal tract, colonic intramucosal carcinoma - defined as dysplastic epithelium that has breached the basement membrane to invade the lamina propria without yet penetrating the muscularis mucosae - carries virtually no metastatic potential. This is because functional lymphatic channels are anatomically absent within the colonic mucosa. The lamina propria of the colon does not contain lymphatics, meaning that tumor cells confined to this compartment have no access to a lymphovascular route of dissemination. Complete polypectomy is therefore curative. Invasion beyond the muscularis mucosae into the submucosa, however, immediately introduces the tumor to an abundant lymphovascular network and constitutes true invasive adenocarcinoma with real metastatic risk. This boundary - the muscularis mucosae - is the single most consequential histologic landmark in the evaluation of a malignant colorectal polyp.
  • Colorectal Lymphoma: A primary or secondary large-bowel lymphoma may present as a mass lesion mimicking adenocarcinoma; immunohistochemistry (CD20, CD3) is essential for distinction.
  • Gastrointestinal Stromal Tumor (GIST): Submucosal spindle cell or epithelioid mesenchymal neoplasm arising from interstitial cells of Cajal; distinguished by CD117 (c-KIT) and DOG1 positivity on immunohistochemistry.
  • Well-Differentiated Neuroendocrine Tumor (Carcinoid): Has roughly equal incidence to adenocarcinoma among small intestinal malignancies; characterized by monotonous cells with granular cytoplasm; positive for synaptophysin, chromogranin A, and CD56.
  • Colonoscopy with Biopsy and Histopathological Examination
  • Mismatch Repair Protein Immunohistochemistry or Microsatellite Instability Molecular Testing
  • Computed Tomography of the Chest, Abdomen, and Pelvis with Serum Carcinoembryonic Antigen (CEA)
  • Iron-Deficiency Anemia as a Colon Cancer Sentinel: Right-sided colon cancers ulcerate and bleed insidiously without altering the appearance of stool, presenting as unexplained microcytic hypochromic anemia. The clinical maxim is that iron-deficiency anemia in any older male or postmenopausal female must be treated as gastrointestinal malignancy until proven otherwise, mandating thorough endoscopic and radiographic evaluation of the entire large bowel.
  • MSI-High Status Is the Single Most Treatment-Altering: MSI-high rectal adenocarcinoma can achieve complete and durable cure with PD-1 checkpoint inhibitor therapy alone, rendering surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy unnecessary in responding patients. Conversely, MSI-high tumors are relatively resistant to conventional chemotherapy. Universal MMR/MSI testing is therefore mandatory before initiating any treatment, and treatment should be deferred until this result is available.

A 62-year-old male presents with three months of progressive fatigue and pallor. He reports no change in stool color or consistency. Investigations reveal a hemoglobin of 8.2 g/dL and a mean corpuscular volume of 72 fL. Colonoscopy reveals a large polypoid, exophytic mass in the cecum. Biopsy demonstrates tall columnar cells with nuclear hyperchromasia and stratification, abundant intraluminal necrotic debris, and a desmoplastic stromal reaction. Immunohistochemistry of the resected specimen demonstrates complete absence of nuclear staining for MLH1 and PMS2 proteins in the tumor cells, with intact staining in the surrounding non-neoplastic epithelium.

  • A. This tumor is expected to respond robustly to oxaliplatin-based adjuvant chemotherapy.
  • B. Activating KRAS mutation is the earliest molecular event in this tumor’s carcinogenesis.
  • C. This tumor demonstrates a dramatically enhanced response to PD-1 immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy and reduced sensitivity to conventional chemotherapy.
  • D. Right-sided location of this tumor confers a better prognosis than an equivalent left-sided lesion.
  • E. The preoperative CEA level is the most critical determinant of this tumor’s therapeutic response

Hepatocellular Carcinoma

16Path

Final Diagnosis

Multifocal Hepatocellular Carcinoma arising on the background of Cirrhosis of Liver


Etiology Pathogenesis Pecularity Differentials Investigations Clinical Pearls Quiz/MCQ Credits
  • Hepatitis B Virus Infection: A deoxyribonucleic acid virus capable of insertional mutagenesis, responsible for approximately 50% of cases globally, rising to 60% in Asia and Africa.
  • Hepatitis C Virus Infection: A ribonucleic acid virus affecting around 70 million people worldwide, accountable for roughly 30% of cases and the dominant cause in Europe and North America.
  • Alcohol Consumption and Metabolic Syndrome: Together responsible for approximately 30% of cases; metabolic dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis is the fastest growing cirrhosis cause in developed nations.
  • Cirrhosis of Any Etiology: Chronic liver damage from inflammation and fibrosis, regardless of underlying cause, creates the carcinogenic field in which hepatocellular carcinoma arises.
  • Hemochromatosis: Associated with germline mutations in the HFE1 gene; one-third of affected cirrhotic patients develop hepatocellular carcinoma during their lifetime.
  • Environmental Carcinogens:Aflatoxin B1, a fungal carcinogen present in food supplies, induces TP53 mutations; aristolochic acid from Chinese herbal medicine also contributes across all etiological groups.
Hepatocellular carcinoma evolves through a well-defined multistep process beginning with precancerous cirrhotic nodules. Low-grade dysplastic nodules progress to high-grade dysplastic nodules, within which mutations in the telomere reverse transcriptase promoter gene emerge in approximately 20% of cases alongside chromosomal gains at 8q. These high-grade lesions then undergo oncogenic transformation upon accumulation of additional genomic hits, with the main molecular drivers being the telomere reverse transcriptase promoter (56%), TP53 (27%), and CTNNB1 (26%), none of which are currently actionable with molecular therapies. The tumor as a whole accumulates 40–60 somatic genomic alterations per tumor, of which 4-8 are considered driver cancer genes. The immune microenvironment and oxidative stress inherent to chronically damaged livers play pivotal roles in inducing these mutations, making hepatocellular carcinoma prototypical inflammation-associated malignancy.
Positive immunohistochemical staining in two of four specific markers - glypican 3, glutamine synthetase, heat shock protein 70, and clathrin heavy chain - is highly specific for hepatocellular carcinoma, enabling pathological confirmation even in morphologically challenging cases.
  • Intrahepatic Cholangiocarcinoma: A primary liver malignancy arising from cholangiocytes with distinct mutational profile including FGFR2 fusions and IDH1/2 mutations; requires immunohistochemistry with K7, K19, and K20 for differentiation.
  • Metastatic Adenocarcinoma: Most commonly from colorectal, breast, or lung primary tumors; hepatocytic markers such as Hep-Par-1, glypican 3, and heat shock protein 70 help distinguish hepatocellular carcinoma from metastatic disease.
  • Mixed Hepatocellular Carcinoma Intrahepatic Cholangiocarcinoma: A rare neoplasm arising from hepatic progenitor cells accounting for less than 0.5% of primary liver cancers; diagnosis is based purely on pathological examination.
  • Four - Phase Contrast - Enhanced Computed Tomography or Multiphasic Magnetic Resonance Imaging
  • Liver Biopsy with Histopathological Examination
  • Alphafetoprotein (AFP) is a 'variable' marker: Small tumors may not secrete it and it can be elevated in non-neoplastic conditions like cirrhosis or massive necrosis. Never rule out HCC solely on a "normal" AFP level.
  • HCC and vascular invasion: HCC frequently invades the portal vein (causing portal hypertension) or the hepatic veins. In advanced cases, the tumor can grow as a continuous "snake-like" column (tumor thrombus) extending through the inferior vena cava and potentially reaching the right atrium of the heart.

A 60-year-old male with a 20-year history of chronic Hepatitis C and cirrhosis presents with sudden clinical deterioration, including rapid development of ascites and weight loss. Ultrasound reveals a new 4 cm solid mass in the right lobe of the liver. Serum Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) is 900 ng/mL. What is the most characteristic clinical or pathologic feature of this tumor?

  • A. Primary dissemination via the regional lymphatic system
  • B. Propensity for invasion into the portal vein or its branches.
  • C. Presence of a central stellate scar on gross examination.
  • D. A better prognosis when associated with underlying cirrhosis.

Multiple Myeloma

16Path

Final Diagnosis

Multiple Myeloma (Clear Cell Variant)


Etiology Pathogenesis Pecularity Differentials Investigations Clinical Pearls Quiz/MCQ Credits
  • Chromosomal Translocations at 14q32: Rearrangements of the immunoglobulin heavy chain locus - t(11;14), t(4;14), t(14;16) - are the most recurrent genomic events; errors in class switch recombination initiates early transformation.
  • Oncogenic RAS Mutations (KRAS / NRAS): Mutations in KRAS and NRAS each occur in approximately 20% of cases, representing the most frequently mutated genes across myeloma genomes and potently activating proliferative cascades.
  • Hyperdiploidy-Chromosomal Trisomies: Gain of odd-numbered chromosomes (3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 15, 19, 21) defines a distinct subgroup in approximately half of patients, generally associated with standard-risk behaviour.
IgH translocation causes Cyclin D1 overexpression leading to uncontrolled plasma cell proliferation, while TP53 mutation/13q deletion confers apoptosis resistance. IL-6 drives continuous marrow proliferation. MIP-1α increases RANKL, causing osteoclast activation, bone resorption, hypercalcemia, and lytic lesions, while Wnt inhibition suppresses osteoblasts, producing punched-out lesions. Excess light chains cause myeloma kidney and AL amyloidosis. Marrow infiltration leads to anemia, thrombocytopenia, and leukopenia. High M protein causes rouleaux formation and reduced normal Ig predisposes to infections.
The pathological hallmark that distinguishes malignant plasmacytosis from benign reactive plasma cell hyperplasia is the emergence of distinctive cytomorphological variants arising from dysregulated immunoglobulin synthesis. These include: flame cells, characterised by a fiery-red cytoplasm resulting from accumulation of PAS-positive glycoprotein; Mott cells, showing multiple grape-like intracytoplasmic immunoglobulin droplets distending the rough endoplasmic reticulum; and cells harbouring Russell bodies (rounded intracytoplasmic globular immunoglobulin masses) or Dutcher bodies (identical immunoglobulin material displaced into the nucleus creating intranuclear pseudo-inclusions). These variants are morphological fingerprints of a pathologically imbalanced secretory apparatus and are not observed in non-neoplastic plasma cell proliferations.
  • Monoclonal Gammopathy of Undetermined Significance: The most common plasma cell disorder, occurring in approximately 3% of persons over 50 years and up to 10% of individuals over 75 years. Distinguished from myeloma by a serum monoclonal protein level below 30 g/L (non-IgM type), bone marrow plasma cells below 10%, and the absolute absence of any myeloma-defining end-organ damage (hypercalcaemia, renal insufficiency, anaemia, or bone lesions). Approximately 1% of patients progress to myeloma annually.
  • Smoldering Multiple Myeloma: An asymptomatic intermediate entity fulfilling quantitative paraprotein or marrow criteria - serum IgG or IgA monoclonal protein at least 30 g/L, or bone marrow clonal plasma cells between 10% and 60% - yet lacking any myeloma-defining events or end-organ damage. Approximately 10% of patients with smoldering myeloma have all three high-risk features, conferring a 76% chance of progression to active myeloma over ten years.
  • Waldenstrom Macroglobulinemia: A lymphoplasmacytic lymphoma producing a monoclonal IgM paraprotein in amounts sufficient to cause hyperviscosity syndrome. Distinguished from myeloma by the absence of lytic bone lesions and hypercalcaemia, a CD138-negative lymphoplasmacytic marrow infiltrate (CD20-positive, IgM-positive), and the presence of MYD88 L265P somatic mutation in over 90% of cases. Bone destruction and significant renal tubular damage are uncommon.
  • Bone Marrow Aspirate and Trephine Biopsy: Clonal plasma cells exceeding 10% of marrow cellularity (typically >30%); eccentric nucleus with coarsely clumped chromatin, basophilic cytoplasm, perinuclear Golgi clear zone; binucleate/multinucleate forms present. Pathognomonic variants: flame cells, Mott cells, Russell and Dutcher bodies. CD138-positive, monoclonal kappa or lambda light chain restriction confirmed by immunohistochemistry or flow cytometry.
  • Serum Protein Electrophoresis and Immunofixation: Narrow monoclonal M-spike in the gamma region; immunofixation characterises heavy chain class (IgG 53%, IgA 25%, IgD 1%) and identifies restricted light chain isotype. Polyclonal immunoglobulins are concurrently reduced. Mandatory 24-hour urine protein electrophoresis and immunofixation required to quantify Bence Jones proteinuria; dipstick and heat tests are unreliable for free light chains.
  • Skeletal Survey and Fluorodeoxyglucose Positron Emission Tomography - Computed Tomography: Plain radiography reveals sharply demarcated punched-out lytic defects (1–4 cm), predominantly in vertebrae, ribs, skull calvarium, pelvis, and femur; osteoblastic reaction is absent. Fluorodeoxyglucose positron emission tomography with computed tomography is now the standard to distinguish smoldering from active disease and detect extramedullary disease. Radioisotopic bone scan is unreliable.
  • Alkaline Phosphatase Paradox: Serum alkaline phosphatase remains characteristically normal in multiple myeloma even in the presence of extensive skeletal disease. This is because the bone lesions are purely lytic with complete osteoblast suppression by dickkopf-1 secreted by tumour cells - no new bone is being laid down. An elevated alkaline phosphatase should prompt investigation for co-existing liver disease or raise suspicion for an alternative bone-forming neoplasm.
  • The Dipstick Trap - Invisible Bence Jones Proteinuria: Standard urine dipstick testing employs a colorimetric albumin-indicator reaction and is entirely unable to detect free immunoglobulin light chains (Bence Jones proteins). In myeloma, the excreted urinary protein consists predominantly of free light chains of low molecular weight (~25,000 Da) that traverse the glomerular filtration barrier freely but are invisible to dipstick methodology. Furthermore, the heat precipitation test is falsely negative in approximately 50% of light chain myeloma patients. A 24-hour urine protein electrophoresis and immunofixation is mandatory for accurate detection and quantification.
  • Serum Beta-2-Microglobulin - The Prognostic King: Serum beta-2-microglobulin is the single most powerful independent predictor of overall survival in multiple myeloma. It represents the light chain of class I major histocompatibility complex antigens shed from the surface of every nucleated cell and thus reflects total tumour burden. Combined with serum albumin, it forms the basis of the three-stage International Staging System: Stage I (beta-2-microglobulin below 3.5 mg/L with albumin at least 3.5 g/dL) carries a median survival of 62 months, whereas Stage III (beta-2-microglobulin above 5.5 mg/L) carries a median survival of only 29 months.

A 68-year-old male presents with back pain, fatigue, and haemoglobin of 8.2 g/dL. Serum creatinine is 2.4 mg/dL and serum calcium is 11.8 mg/dL. Serum protein electrophoresis shows a sharp monoclonal spike in the gamma region. Urine dipstick is reported as negative for protein. Bone marrow biopsy demonstrates 45% replacement by CD138-positive plasma cells with kappa light chain restriction. Which of the following best explains the negative urine dipstick result?

  • A. The glomeruli are entirely spared in multiple myeloma, preventing any urinary protein excretion.
  • B. Urine dipstick detects albumin only and cannot identify free immunoglobulin light chains (Bence Jones proteins). [CORRECT ANSWER]
  • C. Bence Jones proteinuria does not occur when a serum IgG monoclonal spike is simultaneously present.
  • D. Tubular cells in myeloma kidney completely catabolise all filtered light chains, leaving none detectable in urine.
